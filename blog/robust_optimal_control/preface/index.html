Introduction Systems and control objectives


In this series, I will present the notes from my studies in the design of robust and optimal controllers. My goal is to formulate this task through functional analysis: The set of achievable control laws (feedback functions) is explicitly characterized, then an optimal controller is directly synthesized. As such, we are here focusing on model-based approaches in the sense that also the controller is modelled. This differs slightly from other popular approaches: In direct optimal control, we usually search on the space of sequences of control actions (although an explicit control law can be derived in special cases). This approach has the disadvantage that the closed-loop system cannot be characterized, and thus neither the analysis of its performance, stability and robustness. Moreover, I hope to show how we can more naturally study control theory from a game-theoretical, or multi-agent, perspective by focusing on the controller synthesis approach.

In the following, we introduce some notation and present the notion of system (and their models) together with the control objectives which we are interested to solve.

Dynamical (controlled) systems

We consider generalized control systems as represented in the diagram below.

The plant or system (\(P\)) is manipulated by our controller (\(K\)) through the control signal \( u: [0,\infty) \to \mathcal{U}~\subseteq~\mathbb{R}^{N_u} \), which is computed from the output (or measurement) signal \( y : [0,\infty) \to \mathcal{Y}~\subseteq~\mathbb{R}^{N_y}\ \) to produce a desired performance signal \( z : [0,\infty) \to \mathcal{Z}~\subseteq~\mathbb{R}^{N_z} \). Consider these as "real" signals, i.e., signals that can be actually measured and produced by the instruments/components of our systems. Conversely, we assume both the controller and the plant to have internal signals: The system state \( x : [0,\infty) \to \mathcal{X}~\subseteq~\mathbb{R}^{N_x} \) and the controller state \( \xi : [0,\infty) \to {\Xi}~\subseteq~\mathbb{R}^{N_{\xi}} \). These are (latent) signals that we use to describe the dynamics of our systems. Finally, the plant is subjected to a disturbance signal \( w : [0,\infty) \to \mathcal{W}~\subseteq~\mathbb{R}^{N_w} \) representing exogenous factors that affect its evolution. The sets \( (\mathcal{X},\mathcal{U},\mathcal{Y},\mathcal{Z},{\Xi},\mathcal{W}) \) define the spaces for each signal. Formally, we will represent the systems through the LTI models \begin{equation} \tag{1} \begin{aligned} & {\color{gray}\boxed{\text{Controller}}} \\[1ex] & \text{[SS]}\quad \Sigma_K = \left\lbrace \begin{aligned} \dot{\xi}(t) &= A_K \xi(t) + B_K y(t) \\ u(t) &= C_K \xi(t) + D_K y(t) \end{aligned} \right. \\[2ex] & \text{[IO]}\quad K = \left[ \raise-0.2em{ \begin{array}{c|c} A_K & B_K \\ \hline C_K & D_K \end{array} } \right] \\[2ex]\ \end{aligned} \qquad\qquad\qquad \begin{aligned} & {\color{gray}\boxed{\text{(Open-loop) System}}} \\[1ex] & \text{[SS]}\quad \Sigma_P = \left\lbrace \begin{aligned} \dot{x}(t) &= A x(t) + B u(t) + G_x w(t) \\ y(t) &= C x(t) + D u(t) + G_y w(t) \\ z(t) &= Q x(t) + R u(t) + G_z w(t) \end{aligned} \right. \\[2ex] & \text{[IO]}\quad P = \left[ \raise-0.2em{ \begin{array}{c | c c} A & B & G_x \\ \hline C & D & G_y \\ Q & R & G_z \end{array} } \right] \end{aligned} \end{equation} where [SS] stands for a state-space realization and [IO] stands for the equivalent input-output model. For those unfamiliar, a state-space model is simply a system of ODEs describing how the state changes in time, and its algebraic relationship with the measurement and performance signals. The input-output model (a.k.a., the transfer function) is the direct mapping between input and output signals; which, in this context, is equivalent to the Laplace transform of the state-space system (see Appendix A). Here, we must reinforce an important remark: IO models are unique, whereas SS models are not. In fact, this is why I will be mostly referring to \( (\Sigma_K, \Sigma_P) \) as realizations, while denoting \( (K, P) \) as models. Now, a word of caution:

Systems are real entities, but models are always just abstractions. You should never forget that when we refer to the systems through \( (\Sigma_K,\Sigma_P) \) and \( (K,P) \) we are making an assumption, regardless of how good these models are. In this series, we assume those models to be representative of the "real" systems, and we use them to design controllers which are tested through simulation. You should have in the back of your mind that the real system might not behave as our simulations; in fact, that is major motivation for robust methods. In summary, beware of plant-model mismatch.

In these notes, we will assume that all signals and systems are from specific classes of "realistic" functions. That is, we are interested in classes of signals endowed with some description of "energy" or "information", and we focus on classes of systems representing causal mappings. Unless stated otherwise, this will translate formally into the following assumptions

  • \( x,\xi,u,w,y,z~\in~\mathcal{L}_2[0,\infty)\quad \) or \(\quad x,\xi,u,w,y,z~\in~\mathcal{L}_{\infty} [0,\infty) \) \(\quad {\color{gray}\text{(All signals are square-integrable or essentially bounded)}}\)
  • \( P,K~\in~\mathcal{H}_2\hspace{6.9em} \) or \(\quad P,K~\in~\mathcal{H}_{\infty}\quad \) \(\hspace{6em} {\color{gray}\text{(Systems are strictly proper and stable, or proper and bounded)}}\)

The Hilbert space \( \mathcal{L}_2 \) of so-called energy signals represents functions whose norm encodes a notion of energy (or size) which is finite, as is the case of signals describing naturally occurring phenomena. When discussing a signal which does not dissipate its total energy (e.g., periodic signals), we consider the Banach space of (essentially) bounded functions \( \mathcal{L}_{\infty} \). Similarly, the Hardy space \( \mathcal{H}_2 \) represents strictly proper systems which are stable, i.e., that behave as contractive maps. When discussing systems which are only proper and not contractive, but whose magnitude bounds are known, we consider the Banach/Hardy space \( \mathcal{H}_{\infty} \).

System responses, closed-loop responses and discrete-time systems

In the following, we restrict the discussion to the models \( (\Sigma_P, P) \) since everything follows to \( (\Sigma_K, K) \). An important advantage of the linear representation is that we can analytically solve any initial-value problem (IVPs) for the ODEs in Eq. (1). Without loss of generality, let \( w = 0 \) in the following.

The solution of \( \dot{x} = Ax + Bu \) given initial state \( x(0) = x_0 \) is \begin{equation} \tag{2} x(t) = \Phi(t)x_0 + (\Phi B * u)(t), \end{equation} where \( \Phi(t) = e^{At} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} {t^k \over k!}A^k \) is the matrix exponential of \( (At) \).

Assuming \(x_0 = 0 \), we can find the familiar expression \( y(t) = (C\Phi(t)B + D\delta(t))*u(t) \) whose Laplace transform is \( Y(s) = (C\hat{\Phi}(s)B + D)U(s) \). This hints that the matrix-exponential \( \Phi \) characterises the system response in \( \Sigma_P \) completely in the same way that the characteristic polynomial \( \hat{\Phi}(s) = (sI - A)^{-1} \) defines the response in \( P \).
